# Lessons Learned

This page captures my honest reflections on the experience of building this initiative. These are not platitudes — they are observations from having lived through the process, including the parts that were difficult, slow, or unclear.

Three overarching themes emerge as critical for success.

---

## 1. Resilience

<div class="box box-blue">
<strong>The core lesson</strong>
Progress required sustained effort over approximately 18 months, despite uncertainty, institutional transitions, competing responsibilities, and no dedicated funding for the coordination work itself.
</div>

### What resilience looked like in practice

**This was not resolved in a single workshop.** The workshop was the culmination of a year of work: evidence gathering, stakeholder engagement, relationship building, and repeated course corrections. Anyone who expects a single event to solve a systemic problem will be disappointed.

**The timeline was longer than planned.** Initial estimates proved optimistic. Institutional transitions, stakeholder scheduling conflicts, and scope expansion all contributed to delays. These were not failures — they were symptoms of the complexity involved. The initiative survived because the underlying problem remained real and urgent.

**Competing responsibilities were constant.** This coordination work was done alongside — not instead of — active research, teaching, and other professional responsibilities. There was no protected time for it. This is a systemic issue: the research system asks individuals to catalyse change without recognising or resourcing the effort.

### What I wish I had known

- **Build in buffer from the start.** Whatever timeline you plan, add 50%. The delays are not a sign of failure — they are a feature of complex multi-stakeholder work
- **Protect your energy.** You cannot sustain an initiative like this on adrenaline alone. Pace yourself. Take breaks. Accept that some weeks will produce no visible progress
- **Document as you go.** When energy is low, it is tempting to skip documentation. Do not. Your future self — and whoever takes over — will need that record
- **It is acceptable to feel frustrated.** The gap between recognising a systemic problem and seeing any systemic response can be demoralising. That frustration is a normal part of the process, not a reason to stop

## 2. Agility

<div class="box box-teal">
<strong>The core lesson</strong>
The process evolved significantly from initial concept to final workshop delivery. The ability to adapt — to change plans, reframe the problem, and respond to new information — was as important as any initial plan.
</div>

### How the initiative adapted

**The scope expanded.** What began as a question about compute access broadened into a five-pillar framework encompassing governance, careers, IP, authentication, and — critically — training and people. The initiative was never just about infrastructure: the evidence consistently showed that fragmented training, workforce precarity, and the absence of coordination across people and skills were as significant as any hardware gap. This was not scope creep — it was the problem revealing its true shape. The discipline was in structuring the response (five defined pillars) rather than trying to squeeze a complex problem into a simple box.

**The audience shifted.** Early engagement was primarily with peer researchers. Over time, it became clear that institutional leaders, funders, and policymakers were essential. This required a different pitch, different evidence, and different language.

**The workshop design evolved.** Early concepts were closer to a traditional academic workshop — presentations followed by discussion. The final design was radically different: evidence-first, decision-oriented, with structured templates and explicit output requirements. This evolution came from testing ideas, getting feedback, and being willing to discard approaches that were not working.

**The end goal changed.** The original aspiration was a white paper produced directly from the workshop. In practice, a white paper will require additional data gathering and validation beyond what one workshop can provide. Recognising this and adjusting the plan — rather than forcing premature outputs — was the right call.

### What I wish I had known

- **Plan in pencil, not ink.** Your initial plan is a hypothesis, not a contract. Be prepared to revise it substantially
- **Listen for signals that the problem is bigger than you thought.** Expanding scope is not always scope creep — sometimes it is the problem telling you something important
- **Test your assumptions early.** A 30-minute conversation with a potential stakeholder can save you months of work in the wrong direction
- **Embrace iteration.** The first version of your workshop design, your stakeholder map, and your evidence base will be wrong. That is fine. Make them better

## 3. Influencing upwards

<div class="box box-amber">
<strong>The core lesson</strong>
Systemic change required engagement with funders, institutional leadership, and infrastructure providers — not just peer-level collaboration. This is the hardest skill to learn, and the one most likely to determine whether an initiative succeeds or remains aspirational.
</div>

### Why this is different from peer engagement

When you engage with peers, you share a problem. When you engage with senior leaders, you need to offer a solution — or at least a credible path to one. The conversation is fundamentally different:

| Peer engagement | Senior engagement |
|---|---|
| "Do you experience this problem too?" | "Here is the evidence that this is a national problem — spanning infrastructure, training, and people — and here is the economic case for coordination" |
| "We should do something about this" | "Here is a specific proposal with defined costs and outcomes" |
| Shared frustration builds solidarity | Strategic framing builds buy-in |
| Informal, conversational | Structured, evidence-led |
| Time horizon: flexible | Time horizon: "What can you deliver in 12 months?" |

### What worked for influencing upwards

- **Lead with evidence, not emotion.** Senior leaders respond to data, cost analyses, and competitive benchmarking. "Other nations are investing and we are not" is powerful, but "we are each spending six figures on licensing and training that we could share" speaks directly to the financial pressures every university leader is facing
- **Frame the ask clearly.** "We need your support" is too vague. "We need you to sign a concordat committing your institution to participate in a landscape review" is specific and actionable
- **Use the workshop itself as a credibility signal.** The fact that 40+ stakeholders attended, including representatives from national bodies and funders, demonstrated that this was a serious initiative. Senior leaders are more likely to engage when they see that others already have
- **Find institutional hooks.** Connect the initiative to things institutions already care about: strategic plans, REF/research assessment narratives, government policy priorities, competitive positioning, and — increasingly — the need to deliver more with less under severe financial constraint. The economic case for shared resources, coordinated training, and pooled expertise is often the most persuasive hook in the current climate
- **Identify and use champions.** A recommendation from a trusted senior colleague carries more weight than a direct approach from an early-career researcher. Build relationships with people who can open doors

### What I wish I had known

- **Start engaging upwards earlier than feels comfortable.** You do not need to have everything figured out before talking to senior people. They would rather be consulted early than presented with a fait accompli
- **Senior people are busy, not hostile.** The difficulty of getting their time is usually logistical, not political. Make it easy for them: short briefings, clear asks, respect for their diary constraints
- **Written commitments matter.** Verbal support from a VP of Research is valuable. A signed concordat is transformational. Always try to convert verbal support into something documented
- **Do not take institutional hierarchy personally.** If a senior leader does not respond, it rarely means they oppose you. It usually means your email is competing with 200 others. Follow up. Be persistent. Be polite

## Additional lessons

Beyond the three core themes, several specific lessons emerged:

### On community building

- **A "community of doers" is more valuable than a large mailing list.** Focus on identifying people who will actually contribute time and effort, not just express support
- **Diversity of expertise matters more than numbers.** A workshop with 40 carefully selected people produces better outcomes than one with 100 people from similar backgrounds
- **Early-career researchers bring honesty.** They will tell you what is actually broken because they experience it daily. Do not relegate them to observer status

### On sustainability

- **If the initiative depends on one person, it is fragile.** Design for collective ownership from the start, even if one person is driving the early stages
- **Coordination costs money.** The single most common failure mode for initiatives like this is assuming that coordination can happen as a volunteer side-project. It cannot, at least not sustainably
- **The goal is to make yourself dispensable.** The measure of success is not whether you remain central, but whether the initiative continues without you

### On the research system

- **The system does not reward this work.** Building national infrastructure, convening stakeholders, and catalysing collective action are not measured by publications, citations, or grant income. Do it anyway — but be realistic about the career costs, and advocate for recognition alongside the work itself
- **Researchers have a responsibility beyond the lab.** The conditions for doing science — infrastructure, data access, training, governance, career pathways — do not maintain themselves. If researchers do not engage in building and maintaining these conditions, they will deteriorate. This is especially true for training and workforce development, where the gap between what researchers need and what institutions provide is widening

<div class="outcome-banner">
<strong>The most important lesson</strong>
<p>Systemic challenges require systemic responses. Infrastructure, training, and people cannot be addressed in isolation — and none of them can be coordinated through one-off events. The workshop was essential, but it was the culmination of months of work, and the beginning of a longer journey toward shared ownership of resources, skills, and expertise.</p>
</div>
